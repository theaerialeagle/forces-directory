In the Standard Model, the elementary particles are described as 6 leptons and 6 quarks, all of which have corresponding antiparticles. These 12 particles have spin-1/2 and three "generations." Each generation includes two leptons of charges Q = 0 and Q = -1 and two quarks of charges Q = +2/3 and Q = -1/3. Quarks and leptons also have so-called "additive quantum numbers": charge Q, lepton number L, muon lepton number Lμ, tau lepton number Lτ, baryon number A, strangeness S, charm C, bottomness B, and topness T.

Additive numbers Q and A are conserved in strong, weak, and electromagnetic interactions; L, Lμ, and Lτ do not have a role in strong interactions and only apply to electromagnetic and weak interactions; S, C, B, and T are conserved in strong and electromagnetic interactions. Quarks, unlike leptons, also have another additive number referred to as color: red, green, and blue. Because leptons do not have colors, they do not play a role in strong interactions, which are limited to colored particles.
